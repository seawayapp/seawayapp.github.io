Primary 5 Mathematics

The Remainder Concept

Fractions of fractions — understanding what happens to the rest

①

Key Concept

🔢

Part-Whole

The whole is divided into parts. Each part is a fraction of the whole.

➗

Remainder Fraction

A second fraction is taken of the remainder — the leftover part — not the whole.

Key Idea: When the problem says " ⅜ of the remaining students", it means ⅜ × (the remainder), not ⅜ × (the whole class). The remainder is whatever is left after the first group is removed.
②

Example Question

15 of the students in a class like basketball. 38 of the remaining students like badminton and the rest like table tennis.

Given that 12 students like badminton, how many students are there in the class?
③

Method 2 — Branch (Fraction Tree) Method

Multiply Fractions Along Each Branch
1

Find the remainder fraction: after 15 play basketball, the remainder is:

1 − 15 = 45 of the class
2

Multiply along the badminton branch (38 of the remainder):

38 × 45 = 1240 = 310 of the whole class
3

Multiply along the table tennis branch (58 of the remainder):

58 × 45 = 2040 = 12 of the whole class
4

Badminton = 310 of class = 12 students.

110 of class = 12 ÷ 3 = 4 → Whole class = 10 × 4 = 40 students ✓
🏆

There are 40 students in the class.

Both methods give the same answer — choose the one that suits you!

⑤

Summary

🟩 Model (Bar) Method

  • Draw a bar with a common denominator as total units
  • Label each group's units clearly
  • Use the known group to find 1 unit
  • Multiply to get the total
  • Best for: visual learners

🌿 Branch (Tree) Method

  • Split class into fractions at each branch
  • Multiply fractions along each path
  • Find the fraction of the whole for each group
  • Work backwards from the known value
  • Best for: algebraic thinkers
Both methods confirm:  Basketball = 15 × 40 = 8  |  Badminton = 310 × 40 = 12  |  Table Tennis = 12 × 40 = 20  |  Total = 8 + 12 + 20 = 40 ✓
⚠️ Common Mistake to Avoid: Do not take 38 of the whole class. The word "remaining" tells you to first subtract the basketball group, then apply the fraction to what is left. Always read carefully!
